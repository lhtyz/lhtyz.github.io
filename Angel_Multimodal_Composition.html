01
Introduction

Background
& SOAPSTone

S Speaker Louis Dunford is speaking as himself, a working class young man born and raised in North London. He isn't playing a fictional character.
O Occasion Louis Dunford wrote the song as he watched the development of his childhood city. The cobblestones were being torn up, home demolished, and skyscrapers rising in their place.
A Audience The audience are the residents and working class communities of North London. It is the people who grew up in North London who are now watching their neighborhoods being physically erased by developments.
P Purpose Dunford is trying to persuade his audience, the people of working-class North London, that their identity, history, and spirit are permanent and can't be removed like the buildings that have been.
S Subject Place based identity and loyalty. Working class community life in North London. Development and the erosion of cultural history. The tension between the changing physical landscape and the enduring people.
Tone Tone Nostalgic, proud, gritty, and almost defiant with small hints of grief and humor. The tone is overall warm and affectionate toward the community but kind of sorrowful about what is being lost.
02
Argument

Thesis

Louis Dunford acting as the speaker attempts to tell the long-time residents and working-class of North London, mainly those who are watching their neighborhoods be physically erased that their identity, community bonds, and shared spirit are permanent and can't be demolished along with the buildings. They use the rhetorical appeals in the lyrics and the rhetorical appeals in the video to get this message across. After analyzing the lyrics and video, it appears that their use of appeals is effective in convincing their intended audience.

03
L

Lyrics | First Appeal

Logos

Example 01
"The manor might be changing, but the people still remain" / "but the people always last"

End of Verse 1 and Verse 2

Analysis

This is kind of the backbone of the whole song. The argument is this: the physical environment is inevitably changing, but the people who make up its community are more durable than the buildings.

Example 02
"From the Cally to The Cross… The Thornhill to The Hemmy… Arthur's cafe… The Morland and Popham that still stand"
Analysis

The use of real locations here is logos. By rooting his statement in specific places, Dunford presents evidence to support his statement of devotion towards North London.

Example 03
"I see them ripping up the cobbles, and tearing down our childhood homes / I see the architecture changing, watch the history disappear"
Analysis

This is a cause and effect argument. Physical changes lead to cultural erosion. Dunford establishes the threat here, when the buildings go, the history they showed leaves too.

04
P

Lyrics | Second Appeal

Pathos

Example 01
"My blood will forever / Run through the stone"
Analysis

This is the most emotional line of the song. His blood signifies family, sacrifice, life, and death, all in one. This line is pretty much Dunford saying "I belong to you".

Example 02
"It's in the single mothers juggling a baby and a job / in every single brother struggling that wound up in the dock"
Analysis

These lines are designed to show the people who are most vulnerable to the change that are taking place. It is meant to show how the walls being teared down lead to lives being teared down as well.

Example 03
"North London forever / Whatever the weather / These streets are our own / And my heart will leave you never"

Chorus

Analysis

The chorus is an emotional vow. "Whatever the weather" means no matter what condition North London is in, he will remain loyal to it. "My heart will leave you never" signifies love towards his home that will never go elsewhere.

L

Music Video | First Appeal

Logos

Video: youtu.be/wjCJv4W4kvw

Example 01
~0:25 – 1:00

Opening shots of North London streets, market stalls, brick estates, and people going through their daily life.

Analysis

I think the most powerful logos in this video is just showing the people who are a part of the community that Dunford is talking about. The video provides evidence of the effects that are described in the lyrics.

Example 02
~0:25 – 2:00

Contrasting shots of old brick buildings and rising skyscrapers.

Analysis

The contrast between the old and the new is highlighted in these frames. The audience can see for themselves the physical evidence Dunford describes in his lyrics.

Example 03
~2:30 – 3:15

Shots of community members of multiple generations shown in the same streets as the new developments.

Analysis

These shots are evidence of the song's main point: "The manor might be changing, but the people still remain".

06
P

Music Video | Second Appeal

Pathos

Example 01
~0:05 – 1:00

Warm colors used throughout even in footage of modern unchanged streets.

Analysis

The color palette creates an emotional atmosphere of warmth. The style of imagery is used to show that everything being displayed is precious and worth remembering.

Example 02
~1:45 – 2:20

Close up shots of elderly people's faces against the backdrop of the changing streets.

Analysis

These shots highlight the changing environment. The people have aged and become less vibrant while the buildings have been toppled and become meaningless.

Example 03
~3:00 – 3:45

The final shots showing community gatherings, people laughing together, children playing, neighbors talking in the street.

Analysis

The video closes, not with despair, but with warmth. This is meant to leave the viewer with the feeling of joy and resilience for the people who still happily remain and thrive.

07
Final Thoughts

Conclusion

In "The Angel (North London Forever)," Louis Dunford serves as the speaker, speaking as himself, a working-class young man from North London, directly to the residents and working-class communities of North London who are watching their neighborhoods be physically erased by development. The purpose of the song is to persuade his audience that their identity, history, and spirit are permanent and can't be removed like the buildings that have been. Through both logos and pathos in the lyrics, and logos and pathos in the video, Dunford effectively builds both a rational case and an emotional one. The real locations and cause-and-effect arguments give the song its foundation, while the emotional vows of the chorus give it its heart. In analyzing both the lyrics and the video, it appears that the use of appeals is effective in convincing the intended audience.
